The deep and green water,

That’s where, oh where,

You’ll find me, my love,

Atop the old jetty,

Or laid out by the cove,

Where bottles meet the waves,

And tide pulls em out to sea,

“O, by the water,” then started in the crowd after a measure’s rest. They sang, a chorus of motley docksmen, with a hushed and buoying reverence more alike to a holy mass than a pissed tavern at midnight, repeated the song back as dictated, a bit behind the rhythm and well off of the bard’s pitch.

The noise of their chorus took Geld out of his reverie – so beautiful and serene and delicate was the bard’s voice, that it had possessed him out of his native awareness, took him out of this dim and awful-smelling rot pit and into the light and fair gaze of the man’s eyes, into a fantasy protected by the locks of his brown curls, into a land where thoughts and dockworkers and their profuse voices might no longer enter. Geld sighed.

He hummed the melody to himself, and closed his eyes. The chorus stopped as the bard launched into the second verse.

 

My fair darling maiden,

Take my hand in yours

My heart is your oyster

And let these words be your pearls;

Once all my toils and travails

And fortunes have paid due,

On caravel we’ll travel

Out into the blue,

With wind at our sails

And that old world behind,

We’ll throw our worries and cares

O’er the portside!

 

“O, by the water,” started in the crowd again —

That’s right. He was here to steal.

Geld had found of late that the average half-blacked-out patron of the Dancing Lights lacked the same vigilance concerning their belongings that had so proven an obstacle when defrauding the local shopfronts. Moreover, Geld found that once a certain portion of the evening was reached, such matters as their possessions and their peripheral vision and sense of suspicion had drained from them, such that they might assume if they found their pockets lighter they themselves were to blame. There was danger and craft in it, to be sure, but Geld had a light touch and made a light impression, well, light as a blue fifteen-year old with horns and golden blonde hair might make. He found that wearing a big hood tended to help, and so he wore a cloaked tunic into the bar that did well to blur most of his features.

From there, it was simply a matter of sighting a target, passing by quickly but not too quickly, and reaching into their pocket, and with one swift and deft gesture, producing their belongings while still behind them, palming it, and then making towards the—

“Hey, where are you off to in such a hurry, little lady?”

Dread paralyzed Geld in exactly the wrong moment. Had he shifted forward and pushed through the crowd he could have made a scramble out of the bar. But the moment of hesitation and doubt had allowed his brutish victim to land a boot down toward him, and, quite more worryingly now, a grip on his shoulder.

Now a split-second fault had proven fatal to Geld’s entire wit. He should have muscled out of the man’s hold, should have thrown a punch or ducked, but it was no use as he was pulled back like a ragdoll into the man’s view.

He was haggard, bald, middle-aged. Swarthy like every other person in this bar. Tall. Muscled, as Geld could already feel. Geld, unable somehow to make anything but a mistake to abate this catastrophe, met his gaze, which threw his face from a contorted, perverted, confident grin, to confusion, as the man’s thick brows furrowed downward as he stared at his catch. Geld wasn’t sure whether it was that he was a boy or a tiefling that was upsetting him more – but it was safe to assume both proved offensive.

A shrill voice penetrated Geld’s fear, as a figure to the left suddenly slapped the back of Geld’s captor, and revealed a freakish grin.

“Gone and caught a real prize, eh?”

This ‘friend’ burst into hyena-like laughter at his own joke, which was joined in by several of the other patrons who had all been looking on at the scene, apparently. The man’s gaze darted toward his ‘friend’, and the moment of embarrassment caused his grip on Geld’s shoulder, once tight, now to loosen, which was enough for Geld to seize on. He pushed the man’s hold off with his free hand, and ducked to the man’s right, hoping to cut low and weasel away into the tight spaces of the crowd. For a moment he saw a path through the bar, a navigable escape through the sea of uninterested patrons humming along to the music, and felt for a second his confidence come back as he started to run.

Then he felt the glass hit his back.

It was at first a sudden shock to his back like someone had suddenly cut his taut spinal wire apart with scissors. The impact caused him to trip and lose his balance and fall forward. Then there was the explosion of glass. It was being cut by jagged edges in what felt like a million ways in one moment. Blood soaked through the back of his mantle before he even hit the ground.

The bar all around him fell deathly silent in an outward wave. The music, the singing, the talking and laughing and drinking all stopped. He couldn’t help but exclaim in pain as he hit the ground, all of the pain coursing through his body at once.

Their gazes turned from the sound of the impact, to the writhing blue boy laid flat on the ground, to the shards of glass from the shattered stein and the resulting pool of beer on the floor, which spread across the floor as though making its way back to its origin, the bald man, whose fury remained painted across his face like he was frozen in time, still steaming with untempered rage.

Geld could no longer feel anything but searing pain, the cuts from the glass deepening as he attempted to pull himself up, and he groaned again out loud.

The bald man came to an awareness of the unspoken uneasiness around him, the gazes cast upon him that were all unsure what to make of his sudden violence. He looked around to collect their attention quickly, as if sweeping up their eyes and ears, and said,

“That fucking demon nicked my wallet!”

Geld hadn’t even been conscious of the fact that, clutched in his right palm, was the man’s wallet, so concerned he was with escaping – if he had only hidden it properly. And so all who could see him writhing on the ground in pain could plainly see his guilt.

That’s when it hit Geld.

He was going to die here.

He took a look at the small bag of gold that was the man’s wallet. He squeezed it out onto the ground.

Three gold, four copper.

He ought to have been able to laugh. Instead, tears streamed down his cheek. What an unmanly end this would be. Kicked to death by drunkards over a few gold while he couldn’t even pull himself up.

He shut his eyes tight to try to not feel his pain.

The crowd, silent. Then, the bald man thumping forward toward him...

“Hrgk—!”

A gasp echoed through the bar.

Now, that wasn’t quite right.

He opened his eyes, and tried to turn back to the bald man. He stood still, and curiously, there was now a knife embedded deep through his throat.

“The punishment,” came a voice, clear and true from the far side of the bar, “for thievery, is nothing more than a few days in jail.” The source of the voice was marching through the crowd, on light steps.

“The punishment,” came the voice again, now with a twist of mawkish delight, “for interrupting my performances is... Anyone? Anyone care to guess?” He had stopped marching.

Geld heard the feverish strumming of the lute. Everyone heard it, more accurately, as all else was silence.

“DEATH!” cried the bard, and pushed the bald man over. Blood ruptured out of his neck into a massive thick pool on the ground, which intermingled with the spilt beer to create a lake of hideous color and acrid smell.

“What a sorry crowd this is. Not a single laugh. Well, get up, young man, I suppose the show’s over.” Geld felt the bard’s hand on his back. Not grabbing him, but softly touching him. He was tender. A warm glow overtook the pain in his body, as though burning it out. Suddenly, it was as though his wounds had never happened. He felt his body come back to life, having just moments ago accepted death. He took a sharp, shaking inhale of breath, and pulled himself up, and stalked out of the bar in tense silence.

 

“So who are you?” larked the bard. He was idly balancing a dagger on the tip of his finger, keeping a bored eye on it.

“Who am I?” Geld was genuinely taken aback by the question. Perhaps he was so out of sorts to really be taken aback by any question, though. The bard gave him an expectant look. “A thief,” is all he could muster for an answer.

The bard slowly caught the dagger with his other hand and sheathed it, in one fluid motion, then stood up straight and made of show of examining Geld up and down. There was something a bit pronounced and theatrical about everything the bard did, like he walked out of some separate reality, or he found supreme amusement in every second of living life. He remarked after a moment’s deliberation, “Not much of an accomplished one, then.”

“No,” Geld replied, unable to disguise a sudden rising in his throat of acidic bitterness. What was he doing here? Getting interviewed by this madman? He felt hot and angry.

“Well,” the bard yawned, “I wouldn’t know. I’ve never much been able to earn a living myself...” He then looked himself up and down. His outfit was simple, but elegant. Brown leather of some quality, boots, a light green shirt. He certainly stood out in a room of dockworkers as the best dressed, but wouldn’t pass in high society. “I suppose I’m in it for love of the craft,” he said, smiling at the cliche, but completely sincere as well.

“Well, you’re very good,” Geld said, trying as best he could to make the compliment seem like a simple statement of fact rather than an admittance of admiration.

The bard gave him a knowing smile back.

Fuck, he knew!

“I thank you,” the bard offered to clear the second’s silence, “I’m lucky. I didn’t always know what I wanted to do,” he offered quite seriously, all of a sudden, “and I feel quite grateful to know it now.”

Geld nodded. ‘Knowing what you want to do’, he thought. What a concept.

They sat in silence for a moment until Geld felt it was worth a try to address the elephant in the clearing. “Shouldn’t you be going?”

“Why is that?” the bard replied.

“Because, you’ll, hm,” Geld thought. “Well, the man you killed.”

The bard looked up at Geld. “Huh?”

Geld studied him. His mouth opened to form a rebuttal but he was unable to formulate sound. Did he... Forget?

“Oh!” the bard stood to attention. “Yes, yes, now I remember.” The bard showily dusted himself off and carefully strapped his lute behind his back and checked his belongings. Just like that, he was ready to be off again.

He extended his hand to Geld. Geld held it.

“T’was a pleasure meeting you,” and then with a wicked grin, the bard concluded, “thief.”

Marine woke up. The salt air assaulted her nose and tongue. The waves shook her out of slumber’s grasp. Here she was again.

She pawed at her own face and hair for reassurance. Though it was by no means perfection, she felt glad she could discern she wasn’t that scared boy again.

She rose to waking. What had happened last night? She felt her head splitting. The pain the nightmare was at least somewhat real, and here it was. She heard bottles rolling back and forth a bit on her floor, the glass ringing against the hard wood. Fuuuuuuck. She held her forehead in her hands, the forehead just seconds ago she had felt grateful to feel. Now, she felt only pain and regret. What a stupid forehead.

She managed to — slowly— get her feet on the ground and push herself up from bed. Rather distressingly, she had somehow gone to bed stark naked. This wasn’t usually a preference of hers, and usually meant she had taken to some bad and regretful behavior the last night. She had rather not remember, and so tried to think naught of it. What had she done now, she thought. Then:

Who was she?

She balanced herself carefully on the ground, and leaned down to pick up the beer bottles. Then she sat back on her bed for a moment, careful not to lower herself down too much and feel the comfort of sleep again.

She placed a hand on the center of her own chest, felt her heart beat, felt it slow down.

“Only time will tell,” she whispered to herself.

Where was her underwear?

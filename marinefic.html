And I had– I had had– I had had enough of it,” she said, balling her fist a bit instinctually. She noticed it, and was worried about seeming like an unhinged mess in front of the priest.

“Sin doesn’t concern me, child. ‘Tis only the soul that is not at peace with their sin that does.” He gave another warm, comforting grin.

“Right, sin,” she said, her tone giving away her thoughts accidentally. She let silence envelop them for a moment, before remembering she was supposed to speak. Surely, she had more to talk about than piracy or prostitution. She thought about sin.

“A friend of mine…”

Her breath almost caught in her throat.

“A friend I used to have told me, ‘sin was whatever betrays the soul’”. He also said that laying down the footsoldiers of corrupt monarchies and taking back the coin of the people from their coffers is food for the soul, which Marine thought wasn’t worth mentioning. “Like, it was different for every person. I suppose I don’t know if that’s–if that’s actually what’s written down in the Words or not. Is it?”

The old man thought on this, for just a moment. “Tis an astute reading. It’s a tricky thing to pin down. Some are quite vigilant about sin, others believe it to be… how might you put it? A natural part of Creation. That which originates from the soul, originates from the Mother.”

This gave Marine a moment of pause. She remembered quite suddenly that she was a tiefling.

“He was a follower of the Mother, your friend?”

“Yes. He was. He wasn’t a priest, or anything.” Though, Marine thought, this was the first time she had ever spoken to a priest, and it was eerie in certain ways how much this old fart sounded like Gren. Was Gren a priest? She remembered then that priests are supposed to be celibate.

 “He was just… I don’t know if he was really a follower, or, I’m not sure. I don’t really know. I know he read the Words but he read a lot of things. He kept these stacks of tomes and scrolls and he scribbled notes and little things all over them. He believed… probably just as much in any of them.”

“All of Creation is the voice through which the Mother speaks, if one knows how to listen,” the old man said, smiling quite genuinely, and speaking as though he was quoting something.

“Right… I remember him saying something like that, more or less. He, um…”

“He’s with the Mother now?” the old man offered. It took the breath out of her to hear that. She hated hearing it put like that. She would rather have said – his body is fish food at the bottom of the sea. Anything else felt dishonest.

“Almost two years ago, now. Yes.”

“Passing is a part of nature, but that doesn’t make it any easier for the living. You were… close?”

Marine cringed and wanted to leave. She felt a sinking feeling develop in her chest. She remembered everything she had forgotten about why she shouldn’t be in a temple. “Um, I don’t –”

“Tis the Mother’s love that binds all of us, child.” Marine didn’t like being called child, but she was grateful she wasn’t about to be cast out of the temple for – well, a number of things. “These are hard times, and it’s love that can comfort us through those hard times. It is hard to lose that.”

She wanted to cry. “Thank you,” she said, “thank you,” quietly this time. “I miss him. You’re right, it’s – it’s just that things would be so much easier if he were still here.”

“Child, I’ve been here a long time, longer than I care to count. I’ve watched many people exit those temple doors and never come back. I’ve felt this pain you’re going through, no small fraction of it, each and every time. I loved all of those people like they were my own family. Despite their sins, their misgivings. Tis my charge to look after them. To shepherd the Mother’s children. I regret… I’ve failed at this too many times. Too many times to count.” He closed his eyes and smiled in spite of his own words. “Tis a pain… too great for anyone to bear. To watch someone go, knowing they won’t return. I can’t offer any counsel that will make that burden easier.”

His candle had practically withered to its wick. He continued, “Only, know he is at peace now. The same peace you – we all will share with him one day.”

Marine couldn’t help herself from crying through the priest’s words. “Thank you,” is all she could muster in response.

The old man, who had been sat on a stool across from her pew, reached out his wrinkled hand to rest it gently on her arm. She almost recoiled on instinct, but didn’t. She realized no threat was meant from this.

“Only, we are far from that day. You still much more life to live, much more so than myself.”

She winced at this news, self-conscious and feeling overwhelmed by this sudden closeness.

“You’ll be okay,” he said, pulling back his hand delicately as he pulled himself off his stool. “You will find both… pain, and comfort, in the Mother’s world. In her beauty.”

Without much more formality, he announced, “Forgive me, child, but I am old, and need my rest. Stay as long as you like, and you are welcome back here anytime. Day or night.” He blew out the candle, casting the temple back into its gentle darkness, and on light and slow steps, left Marine there in the temple, on the precipice of nothingness. She let tears fall into her hand for a minute longer, then stilled herself. She slung her lute back over her back and proceeded out the arced doors, out unto the dark and awful world.

“You sack of shit,” she spat, “You can’t leave me here. Not like this.” Marine pressed at his wound, attempting to stop the bleeding, and began incanting a curative spell, as her palms lit up with a gentle flame. She stared intently at the site of the rupture, reciting her spell sharply.

“I - I …”

“Errol, you’re going to be okay. You–you have much more goddamned life to live. Much more than me,” she felt her voice trembling. She separated one of her hands from the spell and grabbed his limp arm by its wrist and thrust it into the site of the wound, “Help me, damn it, I can’t do this without you.”

She looked for a glint of that same golden light to reveal itself from his hands. She looked in his eyes, finding him out of focus.

“Errol! Please!” she shouted at him.

“You’re… so beautiful… it’s okay,” he murmured, “this is…”

“You piece of shit! It’s not the fucking time, Errol! Stay with me!” She recited the spell again, feeling the last of the magic leave her body and course into his. His wounds wouldn’t mend. “Damn you, Errol, focus! You can live!” She watched his face start to slow. Start to still.

“NO! FUCK YOU!” She thrust her mouth into his, giving him a long and thrashing series of kisses. When she finally pulled away from him, she watched him smile once more.

“You asshole! Heal yourself! Errol, please, stay with me. Stay with me.”

It was no use. His smile had stilled itself. She thrust her head into his chest and gave a muffled scream.

“You–you fucking asshole.” She pulled herself up, and wiped the blood and tears from her face and mouth. It never gets easier, she thought. You were right, old man.

She stilled herself. She lit a flame in the palm of her hand. She found herself walking, slowly, further into the tunnel, into that darkness, into the beautiful, beautiful world.
